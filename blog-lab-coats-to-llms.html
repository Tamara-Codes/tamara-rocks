From mass spectrometers to LLMs

Tamara Martinovic March 2026 6 min read

I spent the better part of a decade in a lab. Not metaphorically — literally. Gloves, pipettes, centrifuges, the whole thing. My PhD was in biotechnology, and my postdoc focused on bioprospecting the Croatian Adriatic Sea: screening organisms for bioactive compounds, running bioinformatics analyses, trying to extract signal from extraordinarily noisy data.

If that sounds painstaking, it was. Science runs on patience. You design an experiment, wait days for results, and half the time the results tell you nothing useful. Then you redesign, and repeat. The loop is brutal, but it teaches you something irreplaceable: how to think rigorously about complex systems.

"The skills that make a good researcher — systematic thinking, tolerance for ambiguity, obsessive attention to detail — turn out to be exactly what good AI engineering requires."

The pivot

In 2021, I left research to join a diagnostic lab during the pandemic. We were doing high-throughput PCR testing for COVID-19 — hundreds of samples a day, tight turnaround, no margin for error. It was stressful, unglamorous work, but it showed me something I hadn't fully appreciated before: the bottleneck in most complex workflows isn't the hard thinking. It's the repetitive, structured, rule-based tasks that surround the hard thinking.

That's the same problem I'd been watching AI get dramatically better at solving.

I started teaching myself Python seriously. Then machine learning. Then LLMs. I built small automations at first — things that saved me an hour a week at work. Then I started building for other people. Then I went all in.

What actually transferred

People sometimes frame career transitions as starting over. I don't see it that way. Almost everything I learned as a scientist transferred directly:

Debugging is debugging. Whether it's a PCR assay that won't amplify or a LangGraph agent that keeps hitting the wrong node, the process is the same — isolate variables, form a hypothesis, test it, don't fool yourself.

Reading dense technical material quickly. Academic papers and API documentation are both written to be comprehensive rather than readable. Years of wading through journals means I can parse a technical doc in half the time it used to take me.

Working with uncertainty. In research you rarely have complete information. You build the most defensible interpretation of incomplete evidence. In AI engineering — especially when building agentic systems — you're doing exactly the same thing.

What I'm building now

I'm currently at Algorise, building multi-agent AI systems in Python using LangGraph. The goal is intelligent digital workers: AI that doesn't just respond to prompts but actually navigates complex workflows, makes decisions, and hands off to the right system at the right time.

Alongside that, I build and ship my own projects — restaurant menu systems, booking bots, legal automation tools. Small businesses, mostly. The ones that can't afford a full engineering team but absolutely can afford to run smarter.

I'm doing all of this from Croatia, which people find surprising. I find it freeing. The timezone works for European clients. The cost of living means I can take interesting projects over safe ones. And the Adriatic is right there when I need to think.

"I build AI systems the way I used to run experiments: methodically, relentlessly, and with an embarrassing amount of enthusiasm."

If you're building something and think AI could help — or if you're just curious how a molecular biologist ended up writing agent orchestration code — I'd genuinely love to talk.
