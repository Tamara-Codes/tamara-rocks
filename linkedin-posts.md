## February 3, 2025

I can clearly remember the three questions that I was asked at my high school State Final Exam for my informatics class:

Name three peripheral computer parts.
What is the difference between RAM and ROM?
What is BIOS?

Of course, I passed with flying colours, as did everyone else who chose the subject as their elective. The teacher was not invested in teaching, and we spent most of our time playing Counterstrike on LAN. Growing up with a Commodore 64, I fell in love with video games at an early age. But the only contact I had with programming was in primary school, in a class called Technical Education. There, I was introduced to Logo, an educational programming language designed for beginners, known for its “turtle graphics,” where users write commands to move a turtle cursor to create shapes and patterns. At 12 years old though, I didn’t really care for it, I was far more drawn to biology and chemistry, the “stuff of life”. 

My time in Academia spanned over 10 years. Then, by a stroke of luck, Facebook suggested something unexpected: a free, remote, introductory IT online course from a top private university. I became fascinated by the world of binary code, networks, programming languages, databases, UNIX, and more. It was all so new and exciting — like Christmas every day as I unwrapped another piece of the vast and mysterious IT world.

After successfully completing the program, I dove into programming languages, with Python catching my attention in particular. When I first saw the words ‘Hello, World’ appear on my screen after typing just one line of code and pressing enter, I was in awe. It felt like I had just split the atom with my own hands. I no longer craved weekends out with friends; instead, I chose to stay home and learn. Instead of playing video games, I began making my own games. How wonderful is that? 😊

Every line I wrote felt like a tiny victory, and soon I realized I was hooked. 
It didn’t take long before I knew: I had to make a change. The idea of leaving a stable career in science to start from scratch in a completely new field was daunting, but my excitement for programming outweighed my fear. I recognized that this was something I could excel at and genuinely enjoy. The only person standing in my way was myself. If I dedicate myself to studying and working hard, I know I can succeed. If I don’t, I’ll face setbacks — it’s entirely in my hands. I’ve come across countless YouTube videos and Reddit posts from people with diverse backgrounds successfully breaking into the IT field, and each time, I thought to myself, “Why can’t I be one of them?” So, I took the leap and here I am — one of them, a software developer on a journey I never could have imagined when I first sat down at that Commodore 64 all those years ago.

Thank you Dominic Lovric and Luka Miškić for believing in me and giving me the opportunity of a lifetime. I will do my best to excel at Algorise Ltd! 🥳

## June 24, 2025

Why doesn't anyone talk about how fun being a software developer is❓  

I met many people in my life, and rarely have I heard someone say that they love their job. Lawyers, bartenders, teachers, dentists, you name it. For many, work feels like just a means to an end. And trust me, I get it. For two years I was doing a menial job that brought me no joy and was slowly degrading my mental capacities. I started writing code and learning about it in the evenings and weekends, to make my little grey cells stronger again. And it felt good, it felt rewarding. I got hooked on the rush of pushing code to Git, just to see that sweet, sweet terminal output. It felt like I was starring in the movie Hackers (does it make me look old if I reference this? Today's my birthday so I'm emotional 😄). 

Developing software is addictive. So many times have I caught myself thinking 'Just one more try, then I'll stop and put my child to bed.' And then, an hour later, I finally get up because my husband starts yelling at me to get up because it's 9 o'clock AGAIN. 

It used to be video games that captured my free time, now they feel like a waste of time, I'd rather learn and build. Writing software is so much fun it almost feels wrong to get paid for it.
